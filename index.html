Project Scope

Background & Literature Survey

Educational timetable scheduling is a significant combinatorial optimization challenge, crucial for the smooth operation of academic institutions. It involves assigning resources like teaching staff, student groups, and classrooms to a predefined set of events within specific time slots. This task is complicated by numerous hard constraints (e.g., a lecturer cannot be in two places at once) that make a timetable feasible, and soft constraints (e.g., minimizing student idle time) that enhance its quality. The problem is formally recognized as NP-hard. Traditional manual or simplistic methods are often inadequate, leading to suboptimal resource use and dissatisfaction.

The literature explores various computational intelligence techniques. Evolutionary Algorithms (EAs), including Genetic Algorithms (GAs) and advanced Multi-Objective EAs like NSGA-II, MOEA/D, and SPEA2, are widely applied. Reinforcement Learning (RL) approaches such as Q-Learning, DQN, and SARSA, sometimes combined with Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM), offer adaptive solutions. Bio-inspired Swarm Intelligence methods like Ant Colony Optimization (ACO), Bee Colony Optimization (BCO), and Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), along with hybrid methods, have also been investigated. A survey conducted at the Sri Lanka Institute of Information Technology (SLIIT) revealed user dissatisfaction with existing timetables due to issues like clashes, long gaps, and poor communication, underscoring the need for more advanced solutions.

Research Gap

Despite extensive research, a significant gap exists between the capabilities of isolated algorithmic studies and the requirements for a comprehensive, adaptable, and user-centric timetabling solution for real-world educational institutions. Key identified gaps include:

  • Lack of objective cross-paradigm comparisons of advanced optimization algorithms (EA, RL, Swarm) using unified metrics.
  • Inadequate handling of dynamic real-world constraints and the need for rapid rescheduling.
  • Absence of a standardized, comprehensive evaluation framework that considers a wide range of quality metrics beyond simple feasibility.
  • A disconnect between advanced algorithmic potential and practical usability, often due to a lack of integration into functional software with features like Role-Based Access Control (RBAC), validated manual editing, and robust data handling.
  • Limited understanding of the scalability and effectiveness of various algorithms, especially novel hybrid approaches, on large, complex institutional datasets.

Timetable Wiz aims to bridge these gaps by integrating multiple paradigms, providing a robust evaluation system, and incorporating essential practical software features for real-world deployment.

Research Problem & Solution

The Research Problem

The central research problem is the lack of an integrated, adaptive, and comprehensively evaluated timetable system capable of objectively comparing diverse advanced optimization algorithms. This system needs to address real-world complexities, dynamic constraints, multi-objective trade-offs, and incorporate essential user-centric features for practical deployment in educational institutions. Current systems often fall short in one or more of these aspects, leading to suboptimal scheduling outcomes.

Proposed Solution: Timetable Wiz

Timetable Wiz is an Advanced Timetable Generation Solution designed to tackle these challenges. It integrates multiple sophisticated optimization paradigms: Evolutionary Algorithms (NSGA-II, MOEA/D, SPEA2), Reinforcement Learning (Q-Learning, DQN, SARSA, potentially with MCDM), and Swarm Intelligence (ACO, BCO, PSO, and a novel hybrid Ant-Bee Swarm Optimization). A cornerstone of the project is a robust Timetable Evaluation System that objectively assesses schedule quality based on constraint satisfaction, resource utilization, workload balance, and institutional priorities. For practical application, Timetable Wiz incorporates features such as Role-Based Access Control (RBAC), an Extract-Transform-Load (ETL) pipeline for data handling, interactive manual editing capabilities with real-time conflict validation, and potential for API integration.

Research Objectives

Main Objective:

To design, develop, and evaluate an Advanced Timetable Generation Solution that integrates multiple optimization paradigms (Evolutionary Algorithms, Reinforcement Learning, Swarm Intelligence), incorporates a comprehensive multi-criteria evaluation framework, and includes practical system features to effectively address the complex, dynamic, and user-centric requirements of educational timetable scheduling.

Specific Objectives:
  1. Implement and compare diverse optimization algorithms from EAs, RL, and Swarm Intelligence, adapted for educational timetabling.
  2. Develop a comprehensive timetable evaluation system with a multi-criteria module assessing hard/soft constraints and resource utilization, including a weighted scoring mechanism.
  3. Design and implement an integrated software architecture with data handling (ETL), optimization modules, evaluation engine, database, backend API, and an interactive frontend.
  4. Incorporate practical user-centric features such as Role-Based Access Control (RBAC), interactive manual editing with real-time conflict validation, and explore a chatbot interface.
  5. Validate system performance and algorithm scalability using real-world (SLIIT) and benchmark (Muni) datasets, analyzing trade-offs.

Key Results & Findings

Performance Insights & Outcomes

The comprehensive evaluation of Timetable Wiz yielded significant findings:

  • Hard Constraint Satisfaction: Deep Q-Network (DQN) demonstrated superior performance with the lowest constraint violations (2-3 violations), showcasing the power of deep learning in complex optimization. Traditional RL methods like Q-Learning and SARSA also performed well, while Swarm Intelligence and Evolutionary Algorithms showed moderate performance with 3-11 violations.
  • Soft Constraint Optimization: DQN consistently achieved the highest quality scores (89% average) across all soft constraint metrics. Swarm Intelligence algorithms (PSO, ACO, BCO) performed competitively with 82-86% scores, while Evolutionary Algorithms achieved 78-82% performance.
  • Convergence and Learning: DQN showed excellent learning capability and convergence to optimal solutions through neural network-based function approximation. Traditional RL methods demonstrated good efficiency, while MOEAs excelled at generating diverse solution sets.
  • Computational Trade-offs: While DQN required more computational resources (312s, 1650MB) due to neural network training, it delivered superior solution quality. Simpler RL methods remained efficient, and Swarm Intelligence provided a good balance between quality and computational cost.
  • Resource Utilization: All tested paradigms effectively balanced the use of resources (rooms, teachers) and handled structural requirements like lab splitting.
  • System Features: The integrated evaluation framework, Role-Based Access Control, and real-time conflict validation during manual editing proved crucial for enhancing practical usability and bridging the gap between theory and real-world application.
  • Deep Learning Advantage: The research demonstrated that deep learning approaches (DQN) can significantly outperform traditional optimization methods in complex scheduling scenarios, though at higher computational cost. The choice between algorithms depends on institutional priorities regarding solution quality versus computational efficiency.
